The jab is thrown with the lead hand (left hand in orthodox) in a straight line to the target. It is not primarily a knockout punch — its value is in controlling distance, interrupting opponents, and creating openings for the cross and hook.

## Steps

1. Start in your orthodox stance with both hands at your cheeks.
2. Extend the lead hand straight out toward the target, rotating the fist palm-down at full extension.
3. As the jab extends, push off the rear foot slightly and roll the lead shoulder forward to add reach.
4. Snap the hand back to your cheek immediately after impact — do not let it hang out.
5. Return to your guard and reset your stance.

## Key Points

- Speed matters more than power; a fast jab is more useful than a slow hard one.
- Keep the rear hand glued to your cheek throughout — you are vulnerable to a counter cross during the jab.
- Roll the shoulder forward to protect the chin and add a few inches of reach.
- The jab can target the head or be dipped to the body to change levels.
- Throw it constantly — even a light jab disrupts an opponent's rhythm.

## Common Mistakes

- Dropping the rear hand while jabbing, leaving the face exposed.
- Telegraphing by dipping the shoulder or cocking the arm before throwing.
- Pushing through the target rather than snapping back, which slows the return.
- Overextending and leaning too far forward, breaking balance.
- Throwing it as a single punch with no follow-up or head movement.
