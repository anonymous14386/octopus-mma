The uppercut travels upward at roughly 45°, targeting the chin or solar plexus from below. It works best at close range when an opponent is leaning forward, covering up, or has just thrown a punch and left their chin exposed.

## Steps

1. From your orthodox stance, dip slightly by bending the lead-side knee — this loads the punch without telegraphing a big wind-up.
2. Drive upward from the legs and rotate the lead hip forward and upward, as though scooping.
3. The lead arm rises in a tight vertical arc, palm facing you at impact, making contact with the first two knuckles under the chin.
4. Do not let the punch travel past face height — stop and snap back immediately.
5. Return to your guard; the rear hand stays at your cheek throughout.

## Key Points

- The power comes from the leg drive and hip rise, not from swinging the arm up.
- Keep the punch close to the body — a wide arm swing telegraphs and weakens the punch.
- The rear uppercut follows the same mechanics using the rear side and is generally the harder punch.
- At body range, aim the uppercut at the solar plexus or liver rather than the chin.
- The uppercut is most effective when your opponent's head is positioned forward, or after a slip to the outside.

## Common Mistakes

- Winding up by dropping the arm low before throwing, clearly signalling the punch.
- Throwing the uppercut from too far away — it will miss or arrive with no power.
- Swinging the arm wide rather than keeping it close for a compact, fast punch.
- Neglecting the leg drive and rotating only the arm.
- Not returning immediately to guard, leaving the face exposed at close range.
