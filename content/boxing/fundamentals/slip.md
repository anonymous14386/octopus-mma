The slip is a subtle head movement that makes a straight punch (jab or cross) miss by inches. By rotating the torso and bending slightly at the waist, you move the head just outside the punch's path while staying in range to counter immediately.

## Steps

1. As a straight punch approaches, rotate your upper body slightly to the outside of the punch — slip to the outside of a jab by rotating right; slip to the outside of a cross by rotating left.
2. Bend slightly at the waist as you rotate, lowering the head a few inches so the punch passes beside and above.
3. Keep both hands up throughout — the slip is a body movement, not a hand movement.
4. Your weight shifts slightly onto the lead foot when slipping to the right, onto the rear foot when slipping left.
5. Return to centre immediately and counter with the appropriate punch (e.g., a right cross after slipping the jab to the outside).

## Key Points

- The movement is small — a few inches is all you need. Big lurching slips kill your balance and take you out of counter range.
- Keep the chin tucked throughout; slipping with the chin up is an invitation for an uppercut.
- Slipping to the outside of a jab puts you in the safest position and sets up a right-hand counter.
- Slipping to the inside of a cross is riskier but sets up a left hook counter.
- Practise slipping on a double-end bag or with a partner to develop timing.

## Common Mistakes

- Moving the head backwards instead of to the side — this takes you out of counter range.
- Bending at the waist too far, breaking balance and making it hard to counter.
- Dropping the hands during the slip, leaving you exposed to a hook.
- Moving too early (the opponent sees it and adjusts) or too late (you still get hit).
- Slipping without an immediate counter, wasting the positional advantage.
