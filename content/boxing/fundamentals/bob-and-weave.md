The bob and weave is used primarily to avoid hooks rather than straight punches. You drop your level by bending the knees, move the head under the arc of the incoming punch, and come up on the other side in a strong position to counter with a hook or uppercut.

## Steps

1. As a hook approaches, bend your knees to drop your head below the level of the incoming punch — do not just hinge at the waist.
2. Move your upper body in a U-shape: down and to the outside of the punch, then back up on the other side.
3. The entire movement is driven by the legs; the back stays relatively straight.
4. As you come up on the other side, your weight loads naturally for a hook or uppercut counter.
5. Return to a balanced stance immediately after the counter.

## Key Points

- Bend the knees first — a bob that uses the waist rather than the legs is slow and breaks your structure.
- The U-shape should be compact; a wide, exaggerated dip is slow and leaves you off-balance.
- Coming up on the outside of a hook puts you in a dominant position for a right-hand counter.
- Keep both hands at cheek level throughout — your guard does not drop during the bob.
- Combine with a lead hook or right uppercut for maximum effectiveness.

## Common Mistakes

- Bending at the waist instead of the knees, making the movement slow and straining the back.
- Bobbing straight down and up without weaving to the side — you come back to the same danger.
- Moving too far to the side, taking you out of punching range.
- Dropping the hands while bobbing, exposing the face if the punch is followed by a second shot.
- Bobbing predictably at the same rhythm, allowing the opponent to time an uppercut to meet your head on the way up.
