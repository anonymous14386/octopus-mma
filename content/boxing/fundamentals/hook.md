The hook is a horizontal punch that swings in a short arc, targeting the side of the jaw or the liver. Because it travels around the guard rather than straight through it, it is one of the most effective punches for finishing combinations and for slipping inside a cross to land in close range.

## Steps

1. From your orthodox stance, transfer your weight slightly onto the lead foot.
2. Rotate your lead hip and shoulder toward the target simultaneously, pivoting on the ball of the lead foot.
3. Keep the lead forearm parallel to the ground, elbow bent at roughly 90°, and swing the fist in a tight horizontal arc — do not loop it wide.
4. The fist travels along the arc until impact; the knuckles land on the side of the jaw or temple.
5. Immediately rotate back and return the hand to your cheek, keeping the rear hand up throughout.

## Key Points

- Elbow height matters: a hook thrown with the elbow too low becomes a slap; too high and it loses power.
- The power comes from hip and shoulder rotation — a hook with no body rotation is weak.
- Keep the arc tight; a wide looping hook is slow, telegraphed, and easy to see coming.
- The lead hook to the body (liver shot) is executed the same way but aimed downward at the floating rib.
- In combinations, the hook naturally follows the cross because the weight is already shifting to the lead side.

## Common Mistakes

- Looping the hook wide, slowing it and making it visible.
- Punching with only the arm, neglecting the body rotation that generates knockout power.
- Elbow too high, turning it into a swinging slap rather than a punch.
- Dropping the rear hand when throwing the lead hook, leaving the face open to a right hand counter.
- Throwing the hook from too far away — it should land when you are close enough that the elbow is at 90°.
