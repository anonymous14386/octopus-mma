Side control (also called side mount or cross body) is the position where the top player pins the bottom player from the side, with their bodies forming a perpendicular cross. Unlike mount, side control does not use the legs to straddle the opponent — instead, the top player uses chest pressure, arm control, and weight distribution. It is one of the most common positions after a guard pass and acts as a launching pad for advancing to mount, taking the back, or attacking submissions.

## Establishing Side Control

1. After passing guard or from a takedown, position yourself at 90° to the opponent's body — your chest on their chest, bodies perpendicular.
2. Place the near-side knee against their hip to block hip escape.
3. Place the far-side knee or foot against their head or upper body to prevent rolling away.
4. Establish your grips: near arm goes under their head (underhook the near arm or grab the far collar in gi); far arm goes under their far arm or controls their near arm across the chest.
5. Drive your chest into their chest and drop your weight — do not support yourself on your knees.

## Key Points

- The near knee blocks the hip — if they can move their hips, they can begin shrimping and recovering guard.
- Far-side knee or foot blocks rolling — without it, the opponent rolls toward you into guard recovery.
- Drive your chest into theirs: chest-to-chest pressure is the primary control mechanism.
- Your hips should be low and your weight dropped onto them. Supporting yourself on your knees makes you light and easy to escape.
- In gi, the collar and underhook grips provide powerful submission options (americana, kimura, choke entries).

## Escaping Side Control (as the Bottom Player)

- **Shrimp and recover guard** — hip escape by pushing off the mat and turning onto your side, inserting a knee to create space for guard recovery.
- **Bridge and roll** — plant feet, bridge into the opponent and attempt to roll them over.
- **Elbow-knee escape** — create space with frames, recover your knee to the inside, then shrimp to guard.

## Common Mistakes

- Keeping the hips high, supporting the weight on the knees — makes the position light and easy to escape.
- Not blocking the hip with the near knee — the opponent shrimps and recovers guard.
- Allowing the opponent to turn onto their side and face you — this starts guard recovery.
- Trying to submit before securing a stable base — rushing submissions from a loose position creates space for escapes.
