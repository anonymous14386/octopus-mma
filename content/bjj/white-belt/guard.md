The guard is the defining position of Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu and the primary reason BJJ is so effective against larger opponents. When on the back with an opponent between the legs, the guard player is not simply defending — they are actively attacking with sweeps, chokes, joint locks, and positional control. The guard reverses the usual assumption that being on the bottom is losing: a skilled guard player has a significant offensive advantage over an inexperienced passer.

## Closed Guard

The fundamental guard variant. The legs wrap around the opponent's waist and the ankles are crossed behind their back, securing them close and limiting their posture and movement.

1. Lie on your back with the opponent between your legs (or pull guard from standing).
2. Cross your ankles behind the opponent's lower back and squeeze the knees inward to control hip distance.
3. Control the opponent's posture: break it down by pulling the head or collar, or maintain it for attack setups.
4. Keep your elbows in and your hips active — move to maintain the angle of attack.

## From Guard: What You Are Doing

- **Posture breaking** — pulling the opponent's head or collar down so they cannot create space or pass.
- **Hip movement** — constantly angling to maintain a favourable attacking angle.
- **Threatening submissions** — armbars, triangle chokes, guillotines create danger that forces defensive reactions you can exploit.
- **Setting up sweeps** — when the opponent reacts to a submission threat, unbalance them to reverse the position.

## Key Points

- Closed guard is a dynamic, active position — stalling in guard without attacking creates no pressure and allows the opponent to work a guard pass.
- Control the opponent's posture first. Without posture control, attacks are extremely difficult.
- The guard player's hips must stay active — sliding hips out to create angles and elevation is the engine of guard work.
- If the guard is opened (legs uncrossed), you transition to open guard — the guard is not lost, it changes form.

## Common Mistakes

- Lying flat and doing nothing — passive guard invites a guard pass.
- Squeezing only with the legs and ignoring the upper body — guard control involves hands, arms, and posture control as much as the legs.
- Attempting submissions without first controlling posture — the opponent simply postures up and the attack fails.
- Crossing the feet too high (on the back rather than the lower back) — this weakens the control and allows the opponent to stand.
