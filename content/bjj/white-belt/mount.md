The mount (or full mount) is one of BJJ's most dominant positions. The top player sits astride the opponent's torso — hips on chest, knees on the ground — controlling them entirely with body weight. The bottom player cannot generate significant force or leverage, while the top player threatens chokes, arm locks, and strikes. In MMA, the mount is decisive. In BJJ, it scores four points and allows access to high-percentage submissions. Learning to hold mount and escape from mount are both fundamental white belt skills.

## Getting to Mount

Mount is typically reached from side control by walking the knees around the opponent's hip, or from guard passing when the opponent's legs are cleared. It can also be reached from a scramble.

## Holding Mount

1. Sit on the opponent's chest with your hips between their navel and chest — too high (on the stomach) gives them room to escape; too low (on the stomach/hips) reduces your control.
2. Keep your knees pinched inward against the opponent's ribs — do not let your legs flare wide.
3. Stay low: your chest should be near their chest. A high, upright mount is easier to bridge and roll.
4. Establish grips: underhooks, collar grips (in gi), or overhooks to control their arms and prevent framing.
5. Keep your weight on their chest, not on your knees — this is what makes the position oppressive.

## Key Points

- Mount position rewards patience — if your opponent cannot escape, simply maintaining control scores points and exhausts them.
- The low mount (hips close to chest) is more stable and harder to bridge-and-roll than a high mount.
- Knee pressure against the ribs prevents hip-escape (shrimping) which is the most common escape route.
- Grip their arms to prevent frames — if they can get their arms into your hips, they can create space.
- In gi BJJ, the collar is a constant submission threat from mount.

## Common Mistakes

- Sitting too high (on the stomach) — this makes it easy to trap a leg and roll.
- Keeping the knees wide — this makes shrimping and elbow-knee escapes much easier.
- Sitting upright and posting on the ground with the hands — this transfers weight off the opponent and makes bridging effective.
- Ignoring the opponent's arm position — free arms can frame and create space.
- Rushing to submit rather than first establishing a tight, controlled position.
