The triangle choke (Sankaku Jime in judo) is a blood choke executed with the legs. One of the opponent's arms is trapped inside the leg triangle while the other is outside — the asymmetry is what makes the choke work. The thigh presses against one side of the neck while the opponent's own trapped shoulder presses against the other, compressing both carotid arteries and cutting off blood flow to the brain. A properly applied triangle causes unconsciousness in seconds if not released.

## Triangle from Guard

1. From closed guard, break the opponent's posture down so they are hunched forward.
2. Create an angle — shift your hips to one side (e.g., right). Open your guard.
3. Shoot your right leg up and across the back of the opponent's left shoulder and neck. The crook of your knee (the back of the knee) should sit on the back of their neck/shoulder.
4. Simultaneously, clear their right arm to the outside with your left arm — their left arm is inside (between your legs) and their right arm is outside. This one-in-one-out position is the key to the choke.
5. Lock the triangle: hook the right ankle behind the left knee (or behind the right knee crossing it). The leg forms a figure-four.
6. Pull the opponent's head down with both hands and squeeze the knees together while tightening the figure-four.
7. Adjust the angle — rotating your body slightly perpendicular to the opponent's body often tightens the choke significantly.

## Finishing the Triangle

- Squeeze the thighs together to increase compression on the neck.
- Pull the head down with your hands to reduce any gap.
- Push the trapped arm across the centerline (across their body) — this increases pressure and prevents them simply lifting the arm to relieve the choke.
- Rotating 10–15° to face slightly sideways often closes the gap and completes the finish.

## Key Points

- One arm in, one arm out — this is the entire foundation. With both arms inside or both outside, the choke does not work.
- The choke is on the neck, not the throat — it is a blood choke (carotid compression), not a windpipe choke. If the opponent says it's on the jaw or chin, adjust.
- Angle matters — straight-on triangles are often loose. Rotating to an angle almost always tightens the finish.
- Lock the figure-four before pulling the head down — trying to finish without a secure lock is ineffective.
- The trapped arm being pushed across the centreline tightens the shoulder into the neck on that side.

## Common Mistakes

- Having both arms inside the triangle — no compression, no choke.
- Applying the triangle while flat on the back with no angle — very hard to finish.
- Locking the figure-four too loosely (ankle not properly behind the knee) — the structure collapses under pressure.
- Trying to finish with arm strength alone rather than squeezing the legs and adjusting angle.
- Not pulling the opponent's head down — gap above the neck dramatically reduces compression.
