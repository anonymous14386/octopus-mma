The armbar (Juji Gatame in judo) is one of the most fundamental and highest-percentage submissions in Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu. It hyperextends the elbow joint by trapping the opponent's arm across the attacker's hips and applying leverage against the joint. The armbar can be applied from many positions — most commonly from guard and mount at white belt level. It is a tap-or-snap submission: the opponent must tap before the joint is forced past its range of motion.

## Armbar from Guard

1. From closed guard, control one of the opponent's arms — grip the wrist with both hands or one hand, securing it to your chest.
2. Place the same-side foot on the opponent's hip (the side of the controlled arm) to create a posting platform.
3. Shoot the opposite leg up and over the back of the opponent's neck (the leg crosses to the far side of their head).
4. Pivot your hips outward by pushing off the hip-foot, rotating 90° so your body is perpendicular to theirs.
5. Bring the first foot (the hip foot) across and over the opponent's face or onto their shoulder.
6. You should now have both legs clamping the arm: one thigh above the elbow, one thigh across the face.
7. Keep the arm close to your chest, thumb pointing upward. Squeeze your knees together.
8. Lift your hips upward to apply pressure against the elbow joint. The opponent taps.

## Armbar from Mount

1. From mount, the opponent places one arm across your chest to frame or push you away.
2. Trap that arm by pressing your body weight on it and securing the wrist with both hands.
3. Step over to the arm side — move one knee up toward their head on that side.
4. Rotate and fall to that side, bringing your leg over their face.
5. Squeeze the knees, control the arm (thumb up), and lift the hips.

## Key Points

- The arm must be held thumb-upward (elbow crease facing up) — this aligns the elbow joint for hyperextension. Rotating the arm palm-down reduces the effectiveness and risks a shoulder injury rather than an elbow tap.
- Squeeze the knees together tightly — a gap lets the opponent pull the arm free.
- The hip extension (lift) is the submission — the arm across the hip acts as a fulcrum.
- Control the wrist, not just the elbow — wrist control prevents the opponent rotating their arm out of position.
- Tap early if caught — the armbar can hyperextend faster than the caught person can react.

## Common Mistakes

- Allowing the arm to rotate so the elbow faces sideways rather than up.
- Leaving a gap between the knees, which lets the opponent pull the arm out.
- Trying to finish with just the arms (pulling down on the wrist) rather than lifting the hips.
- Not pivoting far enough — if the attacker's body is not perpendicular to the defender's, the geometry of the submission is wrong.
- Cranking too fast — control first, finish with steady upward hip pressure.
