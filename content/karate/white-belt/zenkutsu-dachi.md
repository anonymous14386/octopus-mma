Zenkutsu Dachi (front stance) is the foundational stance of karate kata. Long and deep, it roots the practitioner to the ground and enables the hip rotation that generates power in punches and blocks. In Shotokan, the stance is longer and lower than its Taekwondo equivalent (Apkubi Seogi); in Goju-ryu and Okinawan styles it tends to be somewhat shorter. The front stance appears in virtually every basic kata from Heian Shodan through Bassai Dai and beyond.

## Steps

1. Stand with feet together, then step forward approximately two shoulder-widths — significantly longer than a normal step.
2. Bend the front knee deeply — in Shotokan, the front thigh should approach parallel to the floor (knee above the toes, not past them).
3. Keep the rear leg fully extended and straight — locked at the knee, no bend.
4. The front foot points directly forward. The rear foot turns outward approximately 45°.
5. Push the rear heel firmly into the ground — it must not lift.
6. Distribute weight approximately 60% on the front leg, 40% on the rear.
7. Keep the hips squared forward and the spine upright — do not lean forward or allow the hips to twist sideways.

## Key Points

- The rear leg must be completely straight — any bend in the rear knee raises the stance and reduces its power base.
- The front knee tracks directly over the big toe of the front foot — it must not cave inward.
- In Shotokan, the stance is deliberately very long and low — beginning students consistently make it too short.
- The rear heel stays on the ground throughout. Lifting it collapses the stance.
- Hip rotation in this stance drives the power of both Oi Zuki and Gyaku Zuki — a stable Zenkutsu Dachi enables effective hip rotation.

## Common Mistakes

- Making the stance too short (only one shoulder-width of step) — this prevents the deep front knee bend and loses the grounding effect.
- Bending the rear knee, which shortens the stance and raises the hips.
- Allowing the front knee to cave inward rather than tracking over the toes.
- Lifting the rear heel — this reduces the driving surface and destabilises the stance.
- Leaning the torso forward over the front knee — the upper body remains upright.
