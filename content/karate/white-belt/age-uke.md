Age Uke (rising block) deflects downward strikes — a hammer-fist, overhead club, or descending punch — away from the head by driving the forearm in an upward arc. The technique appears in Heian Shodan and is the standard answer to jodan (head-level) attacks throughout the Heian kata series. The forearm ends above the forehead, angled at approximately 45° so the incoming force is deflected rather than absorbed directly.

## Steps

1. Begin in Zenkutsu-dachi (front stance) or moving stance.
2. Wind up: bring the blocking arm across the body, fist near the opposite hip, palm facing outward.
3. Drive the blocking arm upward in an arc — the forearm rises and crosses in front of the face as it ascends.
4. Finish with the forearm above the forehead, angled forward at roughly 45°. The fist is at forehead height or slightly above. Palm faces outward.
5. Pull the reaction hand (Hikite) sharply back to the hip, palm-up, as the block completes.
6. Kime at the endpoint.

## Key Points

- The finishing position angles the forearm forward at 45° — not horizontal (no deflection angle) and not vertical (blocks vision).
- The blocking surface is the outer forearm (little-finger side).
- In Shotokan, the wind-up starts from the opposite hip rather than the ear-level position common in some other styles. The arc is compact but fast.
- The elbow of the blocking arm should not lock out fully — it remains bent, maintaining structural strength.
- Eyes must remain visible over the forearm — the block is above the forehead, not in front of the face.

## Common Mistakes

- Finishing with the forearm horizontal directly above the head — no deflection angle means a strong descending strike can drive the arm into the head.
- Blocking with a straight arm (no bend at the elbow), which has no structural integrity under impact.
- Covering the eyes with the forearm — the block is above the head, not in front of the face.
- Absent or weak Hikite — the pull of the reaction hand is what generates the speed of the upward drive.
- Raising the shoulder instead of the forearm — the power comes from the rotation of the forearm through the arc, not from shrugging.
