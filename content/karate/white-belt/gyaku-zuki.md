Gyaku Zuki (reverse punch) is the single most powerful hand technique in the karate arsenal and the technique most closely associated with the "one strike, one kill" (Ikken Hissatsu) philosophy of traditional karate. The rear hand crosses the body to strike — the "gyaku" (reverse) meaning the opposite hand to the lead foot. The technique is executed in Zenkutsu Dachi and generates power exclusively through hip rotation: the rear hip drives forward, the shoulder follows, and the arm extends last. Without hip rotation, Gyaku Zuki is just an arm punch.

## Steps

1. Begin in left Zenkutsu Dachi (left foot forward): left fist extended (from Oi Zuki or a block), right fist chambered at the right hip, palm-up.
2. Initiate a powerful hip rotation — drive the right hip sharply forward and the left hip back. This is the engine of the technique.
3. As the hip rotates, drive the right fist forward in a straight line toward the target. At Chudan level (solar plexus); at Jodan level (face) for face punch variation.
4. The fist rotates 180° during the extension — palm-up at the hip, palm-down at full extension. The rotation occurs at the very end.
5. Pull the left fist back sharply to the left hip simultaneously (Hikite).
6. Kime at full extension: the hips, core, shoulder, arm, and fist all lock together for an instant.

## Key Points

- The hip is the origin of power, not the shoulder and not the arm. If you can feel your hip rotating when you punch, you are doing it correctly.
- The fist travels in a perfectly straight line — no arc, no rising curve. The elbow tracks close to the ribs throughout.
- Kime must involve the entire body: the stance, the core, the shoulder, and the arm lock together at the moment of impact. A soft kime wastes all the power generated by the hip rotation.
- The rotation of the fist (palm-down at extension) adds a small torque component but, more importantly, it is a signal that the arm is fully extended and kime is occurring.
- Hikite is not optional — its opposing pull generates the counter-rotation that makes the striking-side rotation faster.

## Common Mistakes

- Punching with only the arm and shoulder, with no hip rotation — the most damaging error, limiting the punch to perhaps 10–20% of its potential force.
- Starting the wrist rotation too early — the corkscrew snap at the very end is what produces the final velocity burst.
- Allowing the elbow to flare sideways rather than tracking past the ribs.
- Punching off a bent rear knee — this prevents the hip from rotating fully.
- Weak Hikite — if the reaction hand is slow or absent, the striking arm is measurably slower.
