The roundhouse kick (Dollyo Chagi) is the defining kick of Taekwondo and the most frequently scored technique in Olympic sparring. The leg travels in a horizontal arc rather than a straight line, striking from the side. The striking surface is the ball of the foot (for precision and penetration) or the instep (for speed and area coverage in sparring with foot protectors). Its combination of speed, range, and the ability to target head-height makes it central to competitive Taekwondo.

## Steps

1. Begin in fighting stance.
2. Raise the kicking knee upward and forward — chamber with the knee pointing toward the target (or slightly past it). The higher the knee rises, the higher the kick can reach.
3. Pivot on the supporting foot, rotating the heel toward the target. This hip rotation is what converts the knee-lift into a horizontal arc.
4. Swing the lower leg outward in a snapping arc from the knee — the foot travels horizontally and strikes the target with the ball of the foot. The ankle is plantar-flexed (foot pointed, toes back) to expose the ball, or dorsiflexed (foot flexed) to use the instep.
5. Snap the lower leg back at contact — do not push through the target, snap into it.
6. Lower the foot to the ground, returning to guard. Do not let it drop; control the return.

## Key Points

- The knee chamber determines the height — chamber low for a body kick, chamber at face height for a head kick. Many beginners try to "lift" the kick at the end; the height is set by the chamber, not the extension.
- The pivot of the supporting foot is essential. If the heel doesn't turn to face the target, the hip cannot rotate and the kick becomes a forward-swinging leg swing rather than a proper roundhouse.
- The snapping retraction at contact multiplies the percussive force and makes the kick harder to catch.
- In sparring (with foot protectors), the instep is the more practical striking surface. In poomsae and self-defence, the ball of the foot is preferred.
- Eyes remain on the target throughout — do not look at the floor when pivoting.

## Common Mistakes

- Not pivoting the supporting foot — the most common error, producing a slow, weak kick with no hip involvement.
- Trying to adjust height by tilting the body sideways rather than by setting the chamber correctly.
- Pushing through the target rather than snapping — this slows the kick and leaves the leg extended and vulnerable.
- Dropping the guard hand on the kicking side during the technique.
- Letting the foot drop after the kick rather than placing it deliberately.
