The back stance (Dwitkubi Seogi) shifts the weight to the rear foot, making the front leg light, free, and ready to kick without telegraphing the movement. It is the characteristic stance of the knife-hand block (Sonnal Makgi) and appears in Taeguek Sam Jang and beyond. Because the weight is already loaded onto the rear leg, a practitioner in back stance can launch a front kick or side kick from the lead leg immediately, without first shifting weight — which makes the technique strategically significant.

## Steps

1. Step forward with one foot so the feet are roughly one-and-a-half shoulder-widths apart (shorter than front stance, similar depth to walking stance).
2. Rotate the rear foot 90° outward so it points perpendicular to the direction of facing.
3. Bend the rear knee deeply over the rear foot — the rear knee should be above the toes of the rear foot.
4. Place the front foot pointing directly forward, with only a slight bend in the front knee — the front leg is mostly extended.
5. Distribute weight: 70% on the rear foot, 30% on the front foot.
6. Keep the hips at roughly 90° to the direction of facing — the body turns more to the side than in front stance.

## Key Points

- The 70/30 weight distribution is fundamental — without it, the front leg cannot kick quickly.
- The rear knee is the loaded, bent knee; the front knee is nearly straight and light.
- The rear foot is at 90° to the direction of the front foot — perpendicular, not at 45°.
- The front foot remains in contact with the floor but bears minimal weight.
- The hips naturally turn about 90° from forward-facing when the rear foot is set correctly.

## Common Mistakes

- Distributing weight 50/50 — this defeats the purpose of the stance and makes quick kicks impossible.
- Setting the rear foot at only 45° rather than the full 90° — this affects hip alignment and reduces the stability of the loaded rear leg.
- Bending the front knee too much, which shifts weight forward unintentionally.
- Making the stance too long (stretching like a front stance) — the depth is closer to walking stance length.
- Allowing the rear knee to cave inward — it should track directly over the toes of the rear foot.
