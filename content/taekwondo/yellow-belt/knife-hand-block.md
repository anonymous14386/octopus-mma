The knife-hand block (Sonnal Makgi) is the first open-hand technique in the Taekwondo curriculum. Unlike the closed-fist blocks taught at white belt, the blocking surface here is the outer edge of the hand (the "knife" edge — from the base of the little finger to the wrist). The technique is characteristically performed in back stance (Dwitkubi) and is distinguished by the simultaneous guard hand held open at solar plexus level, rather than the usual fist-to-hip reaction hand.

## Steps

1. Begin in back stance (Dwitkubi): weight 70% on the rear foot, lead foot pointing forward, rear foot at 90°.
2. Raise the blocking hand (the lead-side hand) to the opposite ear — palm faces outward, elbow bent, fingertips pointing up.
3. Place the guard hand extended forward at solar plexus height, palm upward and open.
4. Execute the block: sweep the lead hand in an arc downward and outward, finishing with the knife edge of the hand at shoulder height on the blocking side, forearm angled slightly downward (about 30° below horizontal). The palm faces away from the body at the finish.
5. Simultaneously draw the guard hand back to solar plexus level, palm now facing upward — the guard hand does not pull all the way to the hip.
6. Kime at the point of completion.

## Key Points

- The blocking surface is the knife edge of the open hand — the outer edge from the base of the little finger to the heel of the hand. Fingers are held together and slightly bent (not rigidly straight).
- The guard hand stays at solar plexus height with the palm open, not pulled to the hip like in fist techniques. This is what makes the technique visually distinctive.
- The block finishes at roughly shoulder height on the blocking side — not at face level and not at the hip.
- The motion is a sweeping arc, not a straight push outward.
- Back stance is required — the weight distribution (70% rear) is part of the technique.

## Common Mistakes

- Pulling the guard hand all the way to the hip — it belongs at solar plexus height, palm up and open.
- Blocking with the palm rather than the knife edge — the wrist must be bent so the edge, not the flat of the hand, contacts the incoming attack.
- Finishing the block too high (face level) or too low (waist level).
- Using a straight-line motion instead of an arc — the wind-up from the opposite ear is essential for generating speed.
- Performing the technique in walking stance rather than back stance.
