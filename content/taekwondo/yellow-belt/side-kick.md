The side kick (Yeop Chagi) is one of Taekwondo's most powerful kicks. While the front kick moves straight forward, the side kick drives perpendicular to the body's facing direction — into the target from the side. The power comes from a hip-loading chamber and a sharp thrust of the hip at extension, not just leg extension. A properly executed side kick can generate enormous force and is effective against both body and head targets.

## Steps

1. Begin in fighting stance or walking stance.
2. Lift the kicking knee up toward the chest — this is the chamber. The knee points directly at the target. The foot is flexed, toes pulled back, heel forward.
3. Pivot on the supporting foot so the heel of the supporting foot turns toward the target and the hip of the kicking leg opens up to face the target.
4. Thrust the kicking leg out in a straight line toward the target, driving through with the heel as the primary striking surface. The hip thrusts forward as the leg extends — this hip snap is the main power source.
5. At full extension, the kicking foot, knee, and hip are aligned in a straight line pointing at the target. The body leans slightly away from the target to counterbalance.
6. Retract the kick back through the chamber position before returning the foot to the ground.

## Key Points

- The striking surface is the heel and the outer blade of the foot (the knife edge) — not the ball of the foot and not the toes.
- The foot must be flexed (toes back, heel forward) at the point of contact.
- Chamber first — the knee comes up before the kick extends. Kicking without a chamber loses both power and height.
- The supporting foot pivots: the heel must turn to face the target so the hip can open and drive into the kick.
- The lean-back is not a mistake — it is required for balance and to allow full hip extension.

## Common Mistakes

- Striking with the toes rather than the heel or blade of the foot — this risks foot injury and loses penetrating power.
- Not chambering the knee first, resulting in a sweeping arc rather than a straight thrust.
- Failing to pivot the supporting foot, which locks the hip and prevents the hip snap that generates real power.
- Extending the kick at an angle (outward rather than straight) — this is a bad habit that limits both power and accuracy.
- Not retracting before setting the foot down, leaving the leg exposed and the balance compromised.
