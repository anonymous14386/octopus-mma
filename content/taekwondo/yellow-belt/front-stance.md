The front stance (Apkubi Seogi) is the deep, long stance of Taekwondo poomsae. Where walking stance (Ap Seogi) is shallow and upright for mobility, front stance is long and low for power generation. The deep knee bend of the front leg and the extended rear leg together create a stable, rooted base. Most reverse punches and many blocks in intermediate poomsae are delivered from front stance, leveraging body weight and grounding to maximize force.

## Steps

1. Step forward so there is approximately two shoulder-widths of distance between the front heel and the rear toes (significantly longer than walking stance).
2. Bend the front knee deeply until the thigh approaches 45° from vertical — the knee should be directly above the toes of the front foot, not caving inward.
3. Keep the rear leg fully extended and straight — locked at the knee, no bend.
4. The front foot points directly forward. The rear foot turns outward at roughly 45°.
5. Distribute weight approximately 60–70% on the front foot, 30–40% on the rear.
6. Keep the hips squared forward, spine upright. The torso does not lean forward over the front knee.

## Key Points

- The front knee must not cave inward (knee valgus) — it tracks directly over the middle toe of the front foot.
- The rear leg is straight and extended — any bend in the rear knee shortens and weakens the stance.
- The stance is significantly longer and lower than walking stance. Beginning students often make it too short, turning it into a glorified walking stance.
- Hips stay squared forward — do not allow the hips to twist toward the rear foot.
- The depth (how far the front knee bends) should allow the thigh to approach 45° without the heel lifting off the floor.

## Common Mistakes

- Making the stance too short, which doesn't load the front leg correctly and loses the grounding effect.
- Allowing the front knee to cave inward — this is a structural weakness and can stress the knee joint.
- Bending the rear knee, which raises the stance and reduces the grounding.
- Leaning the torso forward over the front knee rather than keeping it upright.
- Turning the hips sideways — the hips should face squarely forward throughout.
