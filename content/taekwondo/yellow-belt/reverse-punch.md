The reverse punch (Bandae Jireugi) is one of the most powerful hand techniques in Taekwondo because it uses the full rotation of the hips to generate force. The "reverse" refers to the fact that the punching hand is on the opposite side to the lead foot — in left walking stance, the right hand punches. This cross-body motion allows hip and shoulder rotation to amplify the punch well beyond what an arm-only strike can achieve.

## Steps

1. Begin in left walking stance (left foot forward). The left fist is extended from a previous technique, the right fist is chambered at the right hip, palm-up.
2. Without stepping, initiate a sharp rotation of the hips — drive the right hip forward and the left hip back.
3. As the hip rotates, drive the right fist forward in a straight line toward the target (solar plexus height for a body punch, face height for a face punch).
4. Rotate the fist 180° during the punch — palm-up at the hip, palm-down at full extension. The rotation happens at the end of the motion, not the beginning.
5. Simultaneously pull the left fist sharply back to the left hip, palm-up (reaction hand / Bandae).
6. Kime at the moment of full extension, tightening the forearm, wrist, and shoulder.

## Key Points

- The power source is the hip, not the shoulder. If the hips don't rotate, the punch is arm-only and loses most of its force.
- The elbow tracks close to the body throughout — it does not flare out sideways.
- The wrist rotates at the very end; starting the rotation too early wastes the snap.
- The reaction hand (pulling back the left fist) is as important as the punching hand — it creates equal and opposite momentum.
- In poomsae, the reverse punch often follows a block in the same stance rather than a step.

## Common Mistakes

- Forgetting to rotate the hips — this is the most common and most costly error, halving the potential power.
- Letting the elbow swing wide (winging), which breaks alignment and reduces accuracy.
- Starting the wrist rotation too early in the extension, losing the corkscrew snap.
- Dropping the reaction hand or not pulling it back sharply enough.
- Tensing the arm from the chamber position rather than keeping it relaxed until kime.
