The fighting stance (Gyeorugi Seogi) is the foundational guard position for Taekwondo sparring (Gyeorugi). Unlike the static poomsae stances, the fighting stance is designed for mobility: it allows rapid stepping, quick kicks, and transitions between offense and defense. In Olympic-style WTF/WT sparring, the stance is upright and bouncy rather than deeply rooted, prioritising reach and speed over power generation.

## Steps

1. Step one foot forward (typically the left for a right-handed practitioner, but lead with whichever foot is natural).
2. Turn the body roughly 45° toward the opponent — not square-on and not fully sideways.
3. Place feet shoulder-width apart both laterally and forward-to-back. The stance is shallower than a walking stance.
4. Bend both knees slightly — the weight is on the balls of the feet, not the heels.
5. Distribute weight approximately 50/50 between both feet.
6. Raise both hands into guard: the rear hand protects the jaw and chin, the lead hand is extended slightly in front of the face or chest. Both fists are loosely clenched.
7. Keep the elbows tucked down to protect the ribs. Shoulders are relaxed, not raised.

## Key Points

- The stance is upright and mobile — this is not a deep, rooted stance. You should be able to bounce slightly on the balls of the feet.
- The body angle (roughly 45°) reduces your frontal target area, making it harder for an opponent to score on the trunk protector.
- The lead (front) hand provides distance control and sets up combinations; the rear hand protects the face.
- Keep the chin slightly down and the eyes over the top of the lead fist — never drop the guard to look at the opponent's feet.
- In competition sparring, practitioners often stand more upright and side-on than in poomsae stances.

## Common Mistakes

- Standing square-on to the opponent, presenting the full width of the trunk protector as a target.
- Dropping the hands — the most common mistake in beginners who focus on kicking and forget the guard.
- Planting the heels flat on the floor, making lateral movement and kicks slower to initiate.
- Locking the knees straight, removing the spring needed for quick kicks and evasive steps.
- Holding the lead arm too far extended (arm fatigue) or too close (no range finding).
