The high block (Eolgul Makgi) protects the head and face from descending strikes, such as a downward hammer-fist or club strike. The blocking forearm rises above the head and tilts forward at an angle, so an incoming force is deflected away from the skull rather than driven straight down onto it. Like all Taekwondo blocks, it is a deflection, not a catch.

## Steps

1. Begin in walking stance or fighting stance with hands in the guard position.
2. Bring the blocking arm across the body — the fist starts near the opposite hip, palm inward, like a wind-up position.
3. Simultaneously begin pulling the reaction hand back toward its hip, palm-up.
4. Drive the blocking arm upward in an arc — the forearm crosses in front of the face as it rises.
5. Finish with the forearm above and slightly in front of the forehead, the fist at forehead level or higher. The arm is angled roughly 45° — elbow bent, forearm tilted forward, palm facing outward and slightly upward.
6. Kime (tighten) at the moment of completion. The elbow should not be directly above the head — keep it offset to prevent a strike from driving your own arm into your skull.

## Key Points

- The finishing position has the forearm angled forward at about 45°, not horizontal over the head.
- The fist should be above the top of the forehead, one to two fist-widths above the head.
- The blocking surface is the outside of the forearm (the same side as the little finger).
- Keep the reaction hand sharp and simultaneous — it multiplies the speed of the block.
- Eyes remain level and visible; do not raise the arm so high it blocks your vision.

## Common Mistakes

- Finishing with the forearm horizontal directly above the head — this creates no deflection angle and a strong strike can collapse the arm into the head.
- Blocking with the wrist or fist rather than the forearm.
- Allowing the elbow to straighten fully, removing the spring from the technique.
- Not crossing in front of the face on the way up — the path of the forearm IS the deflection.
- Tensing the shoulder during the rise rather than exploding to kime at the endpoint.
