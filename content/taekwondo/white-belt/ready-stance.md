The ready stance (Junbi Seogi) is the transitional stance used between poomsae sequences and when the instructor signals readiness. It is called at the beginning of a form ("Junbi!") and marks the student as prepared to begin. Unlike the attention stance, the ready stance positions the hands in front of the body, signalling active readiness rather than passive respect.

## Steps

1. From attention stance, step one foot out to the side so the feet are approximately shoulder-width apart.
2. Both feet point directly forward, parallel to each other.
3. Distribute weight evenly — 50% on each foot. Knees are straight but not locked rigidly.
4. Bring both fists in front of the body at approximately belt level (just below the navel), knuckles facing downward (palms facing inward).
5. The elbows are slightly bent and relaxed — arms are not rigidly extended.
6. Keep the upper body upright, chin level, eyes forward.

## Key Points

- The fists are held at belt level in front of the body — not at the hips, not raised to chest height.
- Palms face inward (toward each other), knuckles down.
- Feet are parallel and shoulder-width apart — unlike attention stance where the heels touch and toes splay.
- The stance signals calm readiness — breathing should be relaxed.
- In poomsae, the performer returns to ready stance at the end of the form before bowing.

## Common Mistakes

- Holding the fists at hip level like a hand-on-hips posture rather than in front of the belt.
- Turning the fists so the knuckles face upward or outward.
- Standing with feet too wide (wider than shoulder-width) — it looks relaxed rather than ready.
- Standing with feet at an angle (toed out) rather than parallel forward.
- Allowing the elbows to straighten fully so the arms hang limp rather than positioned.
