The low block (Arae Makgi) is typically the first defensive technique taught in Taekwondo. It protects the groin and lower abdomen against front kicks and low attacks. The technique is executed as a sweeping deflection rather than a direct stop, redirecting force rather than absorbing it.

## Steps

1. Begin in walking stance (Ap Seogi) or fighting stance with both hands in the starting position near the opposite hip.
2. Raise the blocking arm across the body with the fist at shoulder height, palm facing inward — this is the wind-up position.
3. Simultaneously bring the opposite (non-blocking) arm back to hip level, fist palm-up (this is the "reaction hand" or Bandae).
4. Drive the blocking forearm downward in a sweeping arc, finishing with the forearm angled at roughly 45 degrees below horizontal, fist facing down, positioned in front of the thigh.
5. Tighten (kime) at the moment of completion — the block should end with tension, not a relaxed drop.

## Key Points

- The blocking arm must pass in front of the body, not to the side — deflection happens across the centerline.
- Finish with the fist about one fist-width above and in front of the knee, not resting on the thigh.
- The reaction hand pulling back to the hip generates equal and opposite force, increasing the speed and power of the block.
- Keep the blocking elbow slightly bent at the end position — never lock the elbow out fully.
- In poomsae, the block is executed in walking stance simultaneously with a step forward.

## Common Mistakes

- Swinging the arm too wide, leaving the body open before the block lands.
- Finishing too low (on top of the thigh) — the block should be in front of and above the thigh.
- Forgetting the reaction hand — without it the technique is slow and lacks power.
- Over-tensing the shoulder rather than generating power from the hip rotation.
- Locking the elbow completely at the end position, which removes the flex needed to absorb impact.
